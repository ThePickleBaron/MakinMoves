# Backlog: Extreme Momentum = Extreme Reversal (Generic, Reusable)

## Twitter Thread: "The Stock That's Up 300% in 6 Months Will Crash 40% Just as Fast"

Here's why, and how to know WHICH ones survive and keep climbing.

---

Look at any stock that has exploded:
- NVIDIA +[X]% in [timeframe]
- Palantir +[X]% YTD
- Some biotech up 5x on Phase 3 results

Pattern: Stocks with extreme 6-month gains correlate almost perfectly with 20-40% corrections within 12-18 months after the peak.

But here's the catch: Some keep climbing. Others crater.

The difference? **Fundamental divergence.**

---

Three types of 300% movers:

**Type 1: Momentum Bubble**
- Price up 300%, but revenue growth only 20%
- New retail bagholders every week
- Ends: Crash to pre-move levels or lower

**Type 2: Justified but Overshot**
- Price up 300%, revenue up 150%, earnings accelerating
- Correction (30-40%), then resume climbing
- Ends: Healthy consolidation, trend resumes higher

**Type 3: Structural Winner**
- Price up 300%, revenue up 200%+, moat widening, market expanding
- Brief correction, then new breakout higher
- Ends: Continued compounding

---

The edge: **Spot the divergence BEFORE the crash.**

Track: price momentum vs revenue/earnings growth
- If P/S ratio is 50x while comps are at 10x = Type 1 risk
- If P/S at 20x with 200% growth = Type 2 (buyable dip coming)
- If moat is expanding (market share, competitive advantage) = Type 3 (hold)

---

Example framework:
1. Stock up 200%+ in 6 months? Flag it.
2. Check if revenue growth matches price growth.
3. If revenue is lagging price = expect 30-40% correction.
4. If revenue is leading price = correction, then higher.

Position sizing: Assume 40% correction. If you're not comfortable with that, position is wrong.

---

## Context/Why This Works

Momentum attracts dumb money. When retail piles in (as evidenced by extreme price moves without fundamental anchors), eventual exit is violent. But if fundamentals are strong, the correction is a sale, not a crash.

**Key metrics:**
- Price-to-Sales ratio vs industry peers
- Revenue growth rate
- Earnings growth rate
- Market size (total addressable market)
- Competitive moat (network effects, switching costs, etc.)

3. Classify as Type 1, 2, or 3
