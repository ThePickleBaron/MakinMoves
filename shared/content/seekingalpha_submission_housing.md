### ARTICLE BODY (1,200-1,500 words)

**Lead Paragraph:**
The White House announced in February 2026 that "rents hit a four-year low as housing affordability improves." The narrative is technically accurate. Rents, measured by Apartment List's index of new lease asking rents, did hit their lowest point since 2022. But the entire framing is a masterwork of selective data omission. I'm short residential real estate not because housing will crash, but because the market is mispriced for improvement that the data does not support.

**Body Structure:**

**Section 1: The Narrative vs. The Data**

The government's official rent measure (BLS Consumer Price Index for rent) shows the opposite trajectory: up 2.9% year-over-year in December 2025, at an all-time high of 440.667. Same market. Opposite stories.

Why the divergence?

Apartment List tracks asking rents on *new leases only* — roughly 5-8% of the renter population each month. The BLS tracks what *all renters actually pay* — the 92-95% on renewal leases plus the 5-8% on new leases.

The market is pricing the *leading indicator* (new lease rents, down 6%) while ignoring the *lagging indicator* (renewal rents, up 2.9%). The lag mechanism is 12-16 months: when new lease tenants renew their leases (2-year standard), they renew at competitive market rates, which are based on current new lease rates—not their old lease rate.

In the next 12-16 months, one of two things happens:
1. New lease rents rise 4-5% to meet renewal rates, or
2. Renewal rates stay elevated because landlords reset at competitive market rates

Either way, the divergence between leading and lagging indicators must close. The market is pricing a close *downward*. The data suggests a close *upward or flat*.

**Section 2: Supply Wave Is Exhausted**

The White House claims that its housing policies are driving rent moderation. But apartment buildings take 19-24 months from permit to delivery. The current administration took office January 20, 2025. By February 2026 (12.5 months later), the buildings delivering are:

- Permitted in 2023-2024 (previous administration)
- Designed in 2022-2023 (previous administration)
- Financed in 2021-2023 (previous administration)

The supply wave driving 2024-2026 rent moderation was built on the previous administration's policies.

**The Data:**
- Construction starts peaked in 2022 (530K+ units)
- Record completions: 2024 (590K-670K units)
- Current deliveries: 2025-2026 (~500K units)
- Construction starts today: Down 74% from 2021 peaks

**The Implication:**
When current units finish delivering (next 6-12 months), the pipeline will empty. New supply hits its peak, then falls off. With BLS rents already up 2.9%, you're not looking at further relief. You're looking at inflation resuming.

**Section 3: Geographic Divergence**

The White House lists 20 cities with rent declines. The methodology is sound. But the omission is stunning: cities where rents are rising aren't mentioned.

**Reality:**
- Sun Belt metros (aggressive builders during pandemic): rents down 3-6%
- Coastal/Northeast metros (constrained supply): rents up 4-10%

The narrative treats rent decline as uniform. Reality shows it's supply-specific. Where there's new supply, relief. Where there's constrained supply, inflation.

For homebuilders (concentrated in supply-constrained coastal markets), the relief narrative is wrong. Their future rents are in the inflation bucket, not relief bucket.

**Section 4: The Trade Structure**

**Thesis:** Homebuilders are overvalued on supply relief narrative that the data does not support.

**Time Horizon:** 12-16 months (until supply exhaustion is obvious and consensus flips)

**Risk Management:**
- Exit if BLS rents drop below 2.5% YoY (thesis breaks)
- Exit if construction starts spike unexpectedly (supply surprise)
- Hold if BLS rents stay elevated or rise

**Section 5: Why This Matters for Your Portfolio**

This is not a binary bet on housing crash. This is a divergence trade: market prices relief, data prices continued inflation.

If you're long homebuilders, housing REITs, or residential construction suppliers: your thesis is dependent on the supply wave (over) and rent relief (data doesn't support). Review your conviction.

If you're sizing portfolio allocations: consider what happens when consensus flips from "housing is improving" to "housing supply exhaustion = inflation incoming."
