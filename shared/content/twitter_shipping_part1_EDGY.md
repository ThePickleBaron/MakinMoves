Distance jumps: 20K km → 25K km. +5,000 km per voyage.

Same oil, 50% longer delivery. Same number of ships moving LESS cargo because they're slower.

Result: Rates explode from $60K to $150K per day.

Market narrative: "Temporary. Rates collapse when disruption ends."

- Keep pumping → silos overflow (oil sits idle, costing $$ daily)
- Shut wells → lose revenue, risk infrastructure damage

Most shut production down. Wells offline. Silos filling. Supply chain backing up.

---

Fast forward 6 months: silos are AT CAPACITY.

Eastern refineries (China, Singapore, Japan) are starved. Prices spike regionally. Global demand appears suppressed (it's not; it's constrained by logistics).

Market reads this as: "Demand is weak, rates will fall when routes reopen."

WRONG.

---

When the Strait reopens:

Silos have 6+ months of backed-up crude. Wells are offline but ready to restart. Eastern refineries are running at 70% capacity (desperate for supply).

All that oil floods through the strait SIMULTANEOUSLY.

Rates don't crash for 12+ months. They stay elevated or spike AGAIN.

---

Exit: When reopening becomes inevitable and rates spike on actual backlog visibility.

This is what happens when you understand physical constraints instead of price charts.

Silos are full. Wells are offline. Demand is suppressed but not destroyed. Reopening = explosion, not collapse.

Position accordingly.
