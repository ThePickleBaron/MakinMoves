## ARTICLE STRUCTURE THAT WINS

### Opening Hook (50-100 words)
**Pattern:** Start with specific contrarian observation or surprise data point

**Example structure:**
- "The Suez Canal blockade appears resolved, but institutions are still hedging as if it's ongoing"
- "Fertilizer prices have collapsed 60%, yet farm equipment companies are oddly resilient"
- "Central banks are tightening, but emerging market currencies are strengthening — here's why"

**Why it works:** Grabs attention, signals you see what others miss

---

### Thesis Statement (100-150 words)
**What to include:**
1. The first-order consensus (what everyone's priced in)
2. Your contrarian take (what's being missed)
3. The second-order effects (what this cascades into)
4. Time horizon (when does this unfold?)

**Example:**
> Most investors are pricing the Suez blockage resolution as "solved." They're not accounting for the second-order effect: global shipping rates will cascade into freight insurance costs, which cascades into commodity storage costs, which cascades into agricultural futures basis widening. The timeline: 6-8 weeks for full unwind. The opportunity: Institutions are still holding hedge positions that should already be rolled.

---

### Evidence Section (300-400 words)
**What to include:**
1. Data points supporting your thesis (charts, stats, economic indicators)
2. Specific examples (companies affected, sectors impacted)
3. Institutional behavior signals (options flows, fund positioning, analyst revisions)
4. Quote from credible source (Fed official, analyst, news)

**Pattern:**
- Paragraph 1: Quantitative evidence (numbers, % changes, price moves)
- Paragraph 2: Qualitative evidence (news, quotes, institutional signals)
- Paragraph 3: Why consensus is wrong (gap between what's priced and reality)

---

### Second-Order Effects Deep-Dive (300-400 words)
**Critical section — this is where you differentiate**

**What to include:**
1. Primary effect (what changes immediately)
2. Secondary effect (what cascades from primary)
3. Tertiary effect (what most investors miss)
4. Who benefits (companies/sectors that win)
5. Who loses (companies/sectors that get squeezed)

**Example structure:**
```
PRIMARY: Suez clears → global shipping rates decline

SECONDARY: Shipping costs down → ocean freight insurance unwind
           → insurance companies repricing risk models
           → option volatility on freight futures compresses

TERTIARY: Insurance companies repricing → reinsurance flows shift
          → emerging market (re)insurance stocks get bid
          → emerging market currency strength signals
          → capital flows into frontier markets

WINNERS: NAT (tanker owner), shipping ETFs, insurance ETFs, EM currency plays
LOSERS: Logistics companies (margin compression), commodity hedgers (forced to unwind)
```

---

### Actionable Takeaway (100-150 words)
**What to include:**
1. Specific position recommendation (sector, company, instrument)
2. Entry thesis and exit thesis
3. Timeline for position
4. Risk management (what makes you wrong)

**Pattern:**
- "Investors should consider overweighting [SECTOR] because [THESIS]"
- "Specific opportunity: [TICKER] is mispriced at [PRICE] because [REASON]"
- "This position works if [CONDITION]. It breaks if [CONDITION]"
- "Timeline: expect full unwind over [TIME PERIOD]"

## ARTICLE LENGTH + STRUCTURE

**Optimal Seeking Alpha article:**
- 800-1,200 words (not longer, signals better signal-to-noise)
- 5-7 paragraphs minimum (shows depth)
- 2-3 data points or quotes per paragraph (credibility)
- 3-5 specific company/ticker mentions (actionable)
- 1 chart or image (optional, increases views 10-15%)

**Avoid:**
- Articles >1,500 words (bounce rate increases)
- Generic commentary without second-order thinking
- Vague thesis ("market is overvalued" without specifics)
- No timeline (when does this happen?)
- No evidence (opinion without data)
