# How to Build a Macro Conviction Thesis: From News to Position

You see a headline. Oil supply disrupted. Currency collapses. Supply chain breaks. Interest rates spike.

Most traders read it and either panic-sell or ignore it entirely. The headline noise is too chaotic to act on.

But what if you could take that headline and systematically convert it into a portfolio of positions *before* the market prices it in?

That's the difference between reacting to news and *acting on macro divergence*.

Here's how.

---

## The Core Framework: Macro → First Order → Second Order → Position

Your edge isn't predicting the future perfectly. It's mapping the causal chain faster than the market prices it.

**The chain:**
1. **Macro trigger** — Something structural breaks in the world (supply disruption, policy change, geopolitical event)
2. **First-order effects** — The immediate, obvious consequence (prices spike, supply tightens, demand shifts)
3. **Second-order effects** — The cascade (who profits from the first-order change? what breaks next?)
4. **Position** — The companies that win or lose from the cascade

The market prices the macro trigger immediately. It prices first-order effects within days. But second-order effects take weeks or months to price in. That's your window.

## Real Example: The Hormuz Scenario

Let's walk through a real thesis that's playing out right now.

### The Macro Trigger

Strait of Hormuz closure (real geopolitical event, April 2026).

**Immediate facts:**
- 21% of global oil passes through Hormuz daily
- Alternative route (around Africa) adds 2 weeks to transit time
- Affected crude prices spike $20-30/barrel in 48 hours
- Headline writers scream "energy crisis"

**Market response (Day 1):** Oil futures spike. Energy stocks jump. Broad market sells off on recession fears. Volatility spikes. This is all priced in within hours.

### First-Order Effects

Now ask: **What physically must happen if oil supply is disrupted for weeks?**

**Answer:**
1. Existing oil already in transit via Hormuz reroutes around Africa (2-week detour)
2. Tankers run longer voyages = more tanker-days needed for the same volume of oil
3. Tanker companies collect higher day rates because supply of tanker capacity is now constrained
4. Oil-importing nations draw down strategic reserves or find alternative suppliers
5. Crude prices stay elevated until supply normalizes
6. Domestic oil producers (US, Canada, Norway) benefit from higher prices

**Market response (Day 1-3):** Tanker stocks jump (NAT, GNK). Energy stocks rally on higher prices. Shipping ETFs pop. This is relatively obvious — most traders see it by day two.

### Second-Order Effects

Now ask: **If oil supply is disrupted AND tanker capacity is constrained, what else breaks in the supply chain?**

**The cascade:**
- Rerouted oil means longer shipping times globally
- Fertilizer plants in the Middle East sit idle (no feedstock or no exports)
- Global fertilizer supply tightens (potash, nitrogen, phosphate all constrained)
- Food-import-dependent nations face fertilizer shortages within one growing season
- Crop yields drop. Food prices spike. Governments panic-buy. Agricultural commodity prices surge.
- Dry bulk shipping (grain, fertilizer, coal) demand skyrockets because countries frantically source alternative suppliers
- Pipelines inland (US natural gas, Canadian crude) become more valuable because domestic alternatives to Middle Eastern oil matter more
- Reconstruction of Middle Eastern oil infrastructure (after geopolitical tensions ease) requires deepwater drilling rigs and equipment companies

**That's the second-order chain:**

Hormuz closure → Oil supply disrupted → Tankers (first order) AND Fertilizer (hidden cascade) AND Shipping (second order) AND Pipelines (third order) AND Equipment (reconstruction) AND Food Security (final effect)

**Market response (Day 3-14):** Savvy traders notice tankers first. Momentum traders follow. But fertilizer stocks? They don't pop until hedge funds realize "wait, we're about to have a global fertilizer shortage." Shipping companies don't get noticed until aggressive buyers start accumulating. Pipelines don't move much because the connection isn't obvious yet.

**Your window:** Days 3-7, before the second-order cascade is priced in.

## How to Validate Your Thesis Before You Buy

You've identified a macro trigger and mapped the second-order effects. But before you deploy capital, you need to answer: **Is this real, or am I chasing a narrative?**

### 1. Check the Physics

Does the second-order effect actually *have* to happen, or is it possible to avoid?

**Example:** If tankers are constrained because of Hormuz, can the market solve this by using alternative routes that don't require tankers? 

Answer: No. Longer routes require *more* tanker capacity, not less. This is physics-constrained. You can't avoid it.

**Example:** If fertilizer supply is disrupted, can companies instantly produce more?

Answer: No. Fertilizer plants have capacity limits. New plants take 2-3 years to build. This is a real constraint.

**Rule:** If the second-order effect is *avoidable* by human action or substitution, it's not a conviction trade. If it's *physics-constrained*, it's real.

### 2. Check the Valuation

Before you buy, the company must be undervalued *at current prices*, not dependent on your macro thesis being right.

**Example:** NAT (Nordic American Tankers) at $15/share.
- Current earnings: $2.50/share (6x P/E)
- Current dividend: 11.6%
- If tanker rates collapse: Still profitable, still pays dividend, floor is maybe $10
- If tanker rates surge (your thesis): Earnings could hit $5-6/share, stock worth $25+

**The key:** At $15, NAT works even if you're wrong about Hormuz. If tanker rates just return to normal, you still get 11.6% dividend yield while you wait. You're not betting the farm on your macro call.

**Rule:** Never buy a stock that only works if your thesis is right. Only buy stocks that are undervalued *before* your thesis plays out. Your thesis is just the upside.

### 3. Check the Momentum

You've done the macro analysis. The valuation is sound. Now ask: **Is the technical setup confirming or denying the thesis?**

**Example:** CF Industries (fertilizer) at $123.56.
- Macro thesis: Global fertilizer shortage imminent
- Valuation: Trading at 8x earnings (cheap for a $3B revenue company)
- Momentum: Golden cross on the weekly chart. Breaking above 200-day moving average. Volume increasing.

**The rule:** If your macro thesis says "buy" but the chart says "sell," wait. The chart is the real-time market signal. You might be right about the macro, but you're early. Let momentum confirm before entering.

If macro thesis + valuation + momentum all align? That's your entry.

## Building Your Position: The Three-Tranche Structure

Once you've validated the macro, valuation, and momentum, how do you actually build the position?

You don't buy all at once. You build in tranches with OCO (one-cancels-other) stops and targets.

### The Setup

**Example: GNK (Genco Shipping) at $23.32**

You want to hold 195 shares. But you don't want to be underwater if you're wrong. So you structure it in three tranches:

**Tranche 1 (50 shares):**
- Entry: $23.32
- Stop: $22.51 (about 3.5% below)
- Target: $25.50 (9% gain)
- Purpose: Quick profit to pay for the stop loss on all three tranches

**Tranche 2 (45 shares):**
- Entry: $23.32
- Stop: $22.51 (same as Tranche 1)
- Target: $26.00 (11.5% gain)
- Purpose: Second layer of profit if the trade is working

**Tranche 3 (100 shares):**
- Entry: $23.32
- Stop: $22.51 (same as both)
- Target: $28.00 (20% gain)
- Purpose: The conviction hold where your macro thesis plays out

**Math:** If Tranche 1 and 2 hit their targets:
- Tranche 1 profit: $108 (50 × $2.18 gain)
- Tranche 2 profit: $105 (45 × $2.68 gain)
- Total profit from Tranche 1+2: ~$213

This $213 covers the potential loss on Tranche 3 if it hits the stop (100 × -$0.81 = -$81).

**Translation:** You've made Tranche 3 effectively free. You've risked capital on Tranche 1+2 to fund the conviction hold (Tranche 3). If the macro thesis is right, Tranche 3 runs to $28 and you pocket $460 on 100 shares — plus the $213 from earlier tranches. Total: $673 profit on a $4,550 position = 14.8% return.

If you're wrong? Your stop on Tranche 3 limits the damage to the -$81 loss you already offset from Tranche 1+2 profits.

**That's how conviction trades work:** You structure them so the early profits subsidize the core conviction hold. Your stop losses become insurance, not portfolio destroyers.

## The Scalping Rule: Keep 20% Free

Once your thesis starts working — once the position is profitable and you've banked some gains — implement the 20% rule.

**How it works:**

You bought 100 shares of a stock at $50. It's now at $65 (30% gain). You've made $1,500 profit.

Now execute:
1. Sell 20 shares at $65 (harvest 20% of the position)
2. Bank the full profit from those 20 shares ($300)
3. Your remaining 80 shares are now "free" — you've recouped your entire original cost basis and more

**Why this matters:**
- You own 80 shares with zero risk (cost basis is already paid back)
- If the stock goes to $100, you pocket $2,000 more on those 80 shares
- If it drops back to $50, you still made $300 and got out at the peak
- You've separated "profit-taking" from "thesis conviction"

**Example from real trading:**

LITE (Lumentum): You bought 25 shares at ~$80. Stock runs to $112 over a few weeks.

- Profit so far: 25 × $32 = $800
- Sell 5 shares at $112: Bank $560 (covers the cost basis of those 5 shares plus $60 profit)
- Now hold 20 shares with $800 - $560 = $240 "free profit" already locked in
- Your remaining 20 shares are running on house money. If it goes to $150, you make $760 more. If it crashes to $80, you already banked $240.

**The result:** After three-four scalping cycles, you've banked enough profits to own the remaining shares completely free. That's your 20% rule in action.

## The Decision Tree: When to Hold, When to Scale, When to Exit

Once you're in a position, three things can happen:

### 1. Thesis Validates (Price Runs)

**Signal:** Position up 15%+, macro catalyst confirmed (official supply disruption announced, prices spike in commodity markets)

**Action:** 
- Bank 20% of the position (hit your scale target)
- Raise your stop on the remaining 80% to break-even
- Hold the conviction core

**Example:** GNK at $23.32 → $26.80 (15% gain)
- Sell 40 shares, bank ~$160 profit
- Remaining 155 shares still have OCO stops, but you've secured profits

### 2. Thesis Breaks (Catalyst Doesn't Happen)

**Signal:** Macro trigger resolves faster than expected (Hormuz reopens, emergency supplies arrive), OR company-specific bad news (earnings miss, dividend cut)

**Action:**
- Exit immediately on the break
- Take the loss if you're wrong
- Never hold "hoping it comes back"

**Example:** You're holding a fertilizer position because you expect a shortage. But suddenly China announces they're releasing 50M tons of reserves. Shortage thesis is off. Exit the position.

### 3. Thesis Still Unfolding (Waiting)

**Signal:** Macro case is intact, but the position hasn't moved much. No catalysts yet, but the setup is still valid.

**Action:**
- Hold the position with discipline
- Collect dividends if applicable (NAT pays 11.6%)
- Wait for the thesis to unfold over 6-12 weeks

**Example:** You bought CF Industries for a fertilizer shortage that won't hit until the 2026 growing season (4-6 months away). Stock is flat. You just hold and wait for the thesis to validate.

## The Ethical Frame

Here's what separates macro conviction trading from gambling:

**You're not betting on chaos.** You're betting on *who solves the problem.*

If your Hormuz thesis is right and a fertilizer shortage happens, farmers genuinely need fertilizer companies to keep crops alive. You're funding the solution, not profiting from the disaster itself.

This reframe matters. You're not "shorting food and hoping people starve." You're "long the companies that feed people when supply breaks."

It's the difference between:
- **Parasitic:** Betting on disaster to destroy people's lives
- **Symbiotic:** Betting on solutions to save people's lives

The macro thesis framework works either way, but the ethical foundation determines whether you can live with the positions you hold.

## Your Checklist Before You Deploy

Before you buy into a macro thesis:

- [ ] **Macro trigger is real** (confirmed news, structural break, not speculation)
- [ ] **First-order effects are obvious** (can be priced within days)
- [ ] **Second-order effects are non-obvious** (take weeks/months to price in)
- [ ] **Effects are physics-constrained** (can't be avoided by substitution or quick action)
- [ ] **Target companies are undervalued beforehand** (work even if thesis is wrong)
- [ ] **Momentum confirms the setup** (golden cross, breakout, volume spike)
- [ ] **Valuation floor is defensible** (can hold through 40-50% drawdown)
- [ ] **OCO structure is planned** (stops and targets set before entry)
- [ ] **Position sizing is appropriate** (risk capital, not career capital)
- [ ] **Thesis is ethically defensible** (you're funding solutions, not profiting from suffering)
