If you're actively trading across multiple brokerage accounts, you've probably noticed that nobody builds tools specifically for you. Most portfolio trackers are designed for passive investors who check their balance once a month. They don't handle multi-account views. They don't let you document your thesis. And they definitely don't track wash sales.

I spent months testing every major portfolio tracking tool as an active trader managing a six-figure portfolio across individual and retirement accounts. Here's what I found — what works, what doesn't, and which tool fits which type of trader.

## What Active Traders Actually Need

Before comparing tools, let's define what "active trader" means for portfolio tracking purposes. You're an active trader if:

- You hold 15-60+ positions at any given time
- You trade across multiple accounts (individual, IRA, rollover)
- You care about cost basis, sector allocation, and tax implications
- You want to document your thesis for each position
- You need to track realized gains separately from open positions

Most portfolio trackers fail active traders because they were built for someone who owns VTSAX and checks once a quarter. Here's what to look for in a real tracking tool:

**Must-haves:** Multi-account support, cost basis tracking, gain/loss calculation (dollar and percent), sector or category views, and export capability.

**Nice-to-haves:** Thesis notes, watchlist management, tax lot tracking, dividend calendar, wash sale flagging.

**Dealbreakers:** Forced account linking with no manual option, monthly fees over $30 with no clear ROI, lack of customization.

## The Contenders

### 1. TradingView — Best for Charting + Tracking Combo

[Start your free TradingView account →](https://tradingview.com?aff_id=165244)

If you live in charts, TradingView is probably already open on your screen. It's the dominant platform for technical analysis, and it does offer portfolio tracking — but it's a secondary feature, not the main event.

**What it does well:** Real-time price data, community ideas and strategies, alerts on price levels, and the best charting in the business. The portfolio tab lets you track positions with automatic price updates.

**Where it falls short for active traders:** Portfolio tracking is basic. You can add positions, but there's no thesis documentation, no multi-account separation, no realized gains log, and no tax tracking. It's a charting tool that happens to have a portfolio feature — not a portfolio tracker that happens to have charts.

**Pricing:** Free tier is usable. Essential at $14.95/mo, Plus at $29.95/mo, Premium at $59.95/mo. The paid tiers unlock more indicators and alerts, but the portfolio tracking doesn't change much between tiers.

**Best for:** Technical traders who want charting and basic portfolio monitoring in one place. Use TradingView for your analysis and a separate tool for your tracking.

**Verdict:** Essential tool for active traders, but don't rely on it as your primary portfolio tracker.

### 2. Stock Rover — Best for Fundamental Research + Tracking

[AFFILIATE LINK: Stock Rover]

Stock Rover is the tool nobody talks about but every serious investor should know. It combines deep fundamental screening with portfolio tracking in a way that no other platform matches.

**What it does well:** Portfolio analytics are genuinely strong — performance attribution, benchmark comparison, correlation analysis, income projections. The screening tools are best-in-class for fundamental research. And the data depth is remarkable: 10+ years of financial statements for every stock.

**Where it falls short:** The interface feels dated. There's a learning curve. And the portfolio tracking, while powerful, is focused on analysis rather than daily workflow. You won't find thesis notes or trade journaling here.

**Pricing:** Free tier exists but is severely limited. Essentials at $7.99/mo, Premium at $17.99/mo, Premium Plus at $27.99/mo. You need at least Premium for meaningful portfolio analytics.

**Best for:** Research-heavy investors who make decisions based on fundamentals and want deep analytical tools alongside their portfolio. If you pick stocks based on financial statements and valuation models, Stock Rover is your tool.

**Verdict:** Powerful but specialized. Great complement to a simpler daily tracker.

### 3. Sharesight — Best for Tax Reporting + Dividends

[Try Sharesight free →](https://sharesight.com/?ref=INSERT_CODE_HERE)

Sharesight is the portfolio tracker that accountants wish every client used. It's specifically designed for investors who care about tax-efficient trading and dividend income tracking.

**What it does well:** Automatic dividend tracking (including reinvestments), comprehensive tax reports, wash sale detection, unrealized and realized capital gains reporting, and multi-currency support for international investors. It connects to 200+ brokerages for automatic trade imports.

**Where it falls short:** The free plan only supports 10 holdings — that's not enough for any active trader. The interface is functional but not exciting. And there's no thesis documentation or trade journaling.

**Pricing:** Free for up to 10 holdings. Investor plan at ~$16/mo (unlimited holdings, tax reports). Expert plan at ~$24/mo (multi-portfolio, benchmark). Fund Manager at ~$31/mo (professional features).

**Best for:** Dividend-focused investors and anyone who dreads tax season. If you trade frequently and want accurate tax reporting without hiring an accountant, Sharesight pays for itself.

**Verdict:** Best-in-class tax reporting. Worth it if dividends and tax efficiency are central to your strategy.

### 4. Empower (Personal Capital) — Best Free All-in-One

Empower (formerly Personal Capital) is the free aggregator that does the most. It connects to your brokerage accounts, credit cards, loans, and bank accounts to give you a complete financial picture.

**What it does well:** Free is genuinely free — no hidden tier that matters. Account aggregation is smooth. The investment checkup tool analyzes your asset allocation, fees, and diversification. Net worth tracking is solid.

**Where it falls short for active traders:** You can't customize the views. There's no thesis documentation. Positions update on a delay (sometimes hours). The whole experience is designed around passive investing — "here's your allocation, here are your fees, you're doing fine." Active traders need more granularity and control.

**Privacy note:** Empower requires read-only access to your brokerage accounts. Some traders (myself included) are uncomfortable giving any third party access to their financial credentials, no matter how secure the encryption.

**Pricing:** Free.

**Best for:** Investors who want a big-picture financial dashboard across all their accounts without paying anything. Great for net worth tracking and allocation analysis. Not great for active position management.

**Verdict:** Can't beat free. Use it for the big picture, but you'll need something else for active trading.

### 5. Notion (with a Purpose-Built Template) — Best for Customizable Tracking

This is the option I ultimately landed on, and it's probably the least obvious choice on this list.

Notion is a workspace app — think notes meets databases meets project management. It's not designed for investing. But its flexible database system makes it surprisingly powerful as a portfolio tracker, especially for active traders who need features no off-the-shelf app provides.

**What it does well:** Complete customization. You design exactly the columns, views, filters, and relationships you need. Multi-account views, thesis notes per position, realized gains with tax classification, watchlists with target prices, trade journals — all in one workspace. Relational databases mean your positions can link to your realized trades, so when you close something, the full story is connected.

**Where it falls short:** No real-time price feed. You update prices manually (or with API integrations if you're technical). This takes 2-5 minutes daily at market close. If you need live streaming prices, Notion isn't for you.

**Pricing:** Free plan handles everything a portfolio tracker needs. Paid plans ($10-15/mo) add team features you probably don't need for personal tracking.

**Best for:** Active traders who want total control over their tracking system, who value thesis documentation and trade journaling, and who don't mind spending 5 minutes a day on manual updates.

**Verdict:** Most flexible option. Requires setup (or grab a template), but once configured it's the most personalized tracker you'll find.

### 6. Google Sheets / Excel — The DIY Baseline

The spreadsheet. The tool every trader starts with and eventually outgrows.

**What it does well:** Fully customizable (if you know formulas). Free (Google Sheets) or already installed (Excel). Google Sheets can pull live stock prices with =GOOGLEFINANCE(). No third-party access required.

**Where it falls short:** Breaks at scale. Managing 50+ positions across multiple tabs gets unwieldy fast. No relational data — your positions and realized trades live in separate sheets with no connection. The UX is terrible for daily use. And one bad formula edit can cascade through your entire workbook.

**Pricing:** Free (Google Sheets) or included with Microsoft 365.

**Best for:** Casual investors with fewer than 20 positions who are comfortable with spreadsheet formulas. Also good for quick one-off analysis.

**Verdict:** Fine starting point. You'll outgrow it.

## My Recommendation

There's no single "best" tool — it depends on how you trade.

**If you're a technical trader** who lives in charts: use TradingView for analysis, and add a separate tracker for portfolio management.

**If you make decisions based on fundamentals:** Stock Rover gives you the deepest research tools alongside solid portfolio analytics.

**If dividends and tax efficiency drive your strategy:** Sharesight will save you money at tax time. Worth every penny of the subscription.

**If you want a free big-picture view:** Empower gives you the most comprehensive financial dashboard for $0.

**If you want total control and customization:** A well-built Notion template gives you thesis notes, trade journaling, multi-account views, and wash sale tracking that no other tool offers. The trade-off is manual price updates.

**If you're just starting out:** Google Sheets works until it doesn't. Start there, upgrade when you feel the pain.

For what it's worth, I use TradingView for charting and analysis, and a custom Notion setup for daily portfolio tracking and decision documentation. The combination covers everything.
