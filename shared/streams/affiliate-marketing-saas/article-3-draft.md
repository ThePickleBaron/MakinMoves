## Introduction

Three very different tools. Same goal: keep your portfolio organized.

Spreadsheets are free and familiar. TradingView is powerful but pricy. Notion looks beautiful but has a learning curve.

Which one actually wins for tracking a multi-account portfolio?

The answer: it depends on your trading style. But I'll show you the trade-offs, and you can decide.

---

## Option 1: Excel / Google Sheets — The Old Reliable

**Why people use it:** It's free. You probably have it already. And a spreadsheet can do almost anything if you know enough formulas.

### The Pros

- **Total control.** Every formula is yours to edit. Want to calculate cost basis differently? Add a column. Need tax-adjusted returns? You can build it.
- **Truly free.** No subscription. No ads. No company shutting down and losing your data.
- **Familiar.** You've used Excel since college. No learning curve.
- **Works everywhere.** Windows, Mac, phone (Google Sheets), web, desktop.

### The Cons

- **Manual data entry.** If you have 50 positions across 3 accounts, you're updating this spreadsheet manually. Every day. Or paying $30+ for a data-feed add-on.
- **No real-time prices.** Your Excel file was updated at noon. Your portfolio moved 2% by 3pm. You don't know until you refresh tomorrow.
- **Breaks at scale.** Works great until you have 100 positions. Then it gets slow. Formulas break. Updates take forever.
- **Ugly.** Rows and columns. Not much else to say.
- **Version control nightmare.** Email the file to yourself? Multiple versions everywhere. Did you update Tuesday's file or Friday's?

### Best For

Casual investors with <20 positions. You check your portfolio monthly. You don't need real-time data.

### Pricing

Free (or $99/year for Microsoft 365, which you probably already have).

## Option 2: TradingView — The Power Tool

**Why people use it:** It's where professional traders live. Charts, alerts, community, backtesting. Portfolio tracking is secondary, but it's built-in.

### The Pros

- **Industry standard.** If you ask a trader where they track their portfolio, half will say TradingView.
- **Real-time data.** Every tick. Every price movement. Live updates.
- **Charting integration.** Your portfolio is built on the best charting platform in the world. Zoom into your positions instantly.
- **Alerts.** Price alerts, technical alerts, portfolio alerts. Set them and forget.
- **Community.** See what other traders are watching. Idea flow.
- **Mobile app.** Check portfolio on the go with live data.

### The Cons

- **Portfolio tracking is secondary.** TradingView is designed for charting and analysis. Portfolio tracking is just... there. Not optimized.
- **Multi-account support is weak.** You can track positions, but it gets messy with multiple accounts (brokerage, IRA, crypto).
- **Paid tier for full features.** The free version works, but real-time data, advanced charting, and alerts require paid ($15–$60/month depending on tier).
- **Not great for notes/thesis.** You can add labels, but there's no structured "thesis notes" or "why I bought this" tracking.
- **Overkill if you're not analyzing.** If you just want to know your positions and allocation, you're paying for features you won't use.

### Best For

Technical traders who spend their day in charts anyway. You already have TradingView open, so your portfolio is right there.

### Pricing

Free for basic tracking. $15–$60/month for paid tiers with real-time data and advanced features.

## Option 3: Notion — The Customizable Middle Ground

**Why people use it:** It's a flexible database wrapped in a beautiful UI. You can build exactly what you need, no coding.

### The Pros

- **Relational databases.** Link your positions to accounts, accounts to institutions, institutions to contact info. Organize however you want.
- **Multiple views of the same data.** See your portfolio by account, by sector, by thesis, by time held. All views pull from the same source.
- **Structured thesis notes.** "Why did I buy this? When would I sell?" Not just a comment, but a real database field you can filter on.
- **Mobile-friendly.** Not ideal for charting, but checking your positions on the go works great.
- **Beautiful.** Notion looks professional. Your portfolio tracking feels like a real system, not a spreadsheet.
- **Zero cost.** Notion's free tier handles a portfolio database easily.

### The Cons

- **No real-time price feeds.** You either enter prices manually, or use an API (requires technical setup).
- **Learning curve.** If you've never used Notion, the first setup takes 1–2 hours. Relational databases aren't intuitive if you're used to spreadsheets.
- **Not built for charting.** If you want to see a price chart, you're switching to TradingView anyway.
- **Requires discipline.** It's a tool, not a system. You have to actually use it consistently or data gets stale.

### Best For

Active traders who want a personalized system. You care about context (thesis, entry date, conviction), not just real-time prices. You're willing to spend 10 minutes a day updating positions.

### Pricing

Free (up to 1,000 blocks, which handles most portfolios). Premium plan is $10/month if you need unlimited storage.

## The Verdict

**If you want real-time data and you live in charts:** TradingView. Accept that portfolio tracking is secondary and set it up within their system.

**If you want total control and zero cost:** Excel / Google Sheets. Accept that you'll update manually.

**If you want a personal system with context:** Notion. Accept that you'll manage data entry.

---

## The Hybrid Approach (My Recommendation)

Use all three. Not as much as it sounds.

- **TradingView:** Your charting and real-time analysis platform. Do your research, place your trades.
- **Notion:** Your portfolio database. Positions, accounts, thesis notes, thesis updates, tracking results.
- **Excel:** Export-import point (when moving data between systems, or for tax prep).

Here's how it works:

1. You analyze and buy a position in TradingView.
2. You add the position to your Notion database (including thesis, entry date, conviction).
3. Each week or month, you review your positions in Notion and update thesis notes.
4. At year-end, you export your Notion positions to Excel for tax prep or send to your accountant.

With this approach, you get:
- Real-time charting (TradingView)
- Personalized tracking and context (Notion)
- Clean data for tax prep (Excel)
- None of the drawbacks of using just one tool

## Final Thought

Your portfolio tracker should match your trading style.

- Do hourly analysis? Use TradingView.
- Do weekly review? Use Notion (or a spreadsheet if you prefer simplicity).
- Do quarterly rebalancing? Either works. Pick whichever is simpler.
