## TEMPLATE 1: MACRO DIVERGENCE DETECTOR

### Purpose
Identify macro divergences in real-time. Framework forces you to articulate:
- What the market is pricing
- What you're pricing instead
- Why there's a gap
- How confident you are

### Structure + Sections

**Section 1: Trigger Input**
- Macro event (news, policy change, supply shock)
- Date triggered
- Public narrative (what media is saying)

**Section 2: Institution's Narrative** (What the market is pricing)
- Headline thesis (e.g., "Soft landing, Fed cuts coming")
- Institutional positioning (what big money bought/sold)
- How market is pricing this (e.g., equities rallied, rates fell)
- Confidence level (markets look convinced or uncertain?)

**Section 3: Your Thesis** (The divergence you see)
- What institutions are missing
- Your alternative narrative
- Why you think you're right
- Data/evidence supporting your view

**Section 4: Timeline**
- When this divergence might close
- Short-term (0–3 months)
- Medium-term (3–12 months)
- Long-term (12+ months)

**Section 5: Conviction Weight**
- Scale 1–10 (how sure are you?)
- Factors that could break your thesis
- What would validate your thesis

**Section 6: Next Steps**
- Do you trade this now, or watch?
- If you trade: What's your thesis position?
- Checkpoints (dates/data to monitor)

### Example Data (Hormuz Closure)

| Field | Data |
|-------|------|
| **Trigger** | Hormuz chokepoint closure (geopolitical tension, April 2026) |
| **Institution Narrative** | Oil prices spike; shipping costs increase 5–15%; energy stocks rally; market moves on |
| **Your Thesis** | Supply shock cascades to fertilizer shortage, then equipment replacement cycle. Market hasn't priced cascade yet. Equipment manufacturers undervalued. |
| **Timeline** | Divergence closes over 6–12 months as cascade unfolds |
| **Conviction** | 8/10 (supply shocks always cascade; only question is speed) |
| **Thesis Position** | Long equipment manufacturers (John Deere, AGCO); monitor fertilizer prices |

### Walkthrough Guide (3 Paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1: How to Fill It Out**

Start with the trigger. What changed? A supply shock? A policy shift? A earnings surprise? Write one sentence. Then write the headline narrative—what every financial media outlet is talking about. That's what institutions are pricing. Now write what you think is different. Don't overthink it. What are they missing?

**Paragraph 2: The Conviction Weighting**

Rate your confidence 1–10. If you're below 5, don't trade it. If you're 7+, this is a core conviction. Use the conviction weight in your position sizing. You'll use this later in the Thesis Execution Planner.

**Paragraph 3: Using It Over Time**

Fill this out when the trigger happens. Then revisit it monthly. Does your thesis still hold? Did the market start pricing the cascade? Did new data change your view? Update the template as your thesis evolves. You're documenting your thinking, not locking it in stone.

## TEMPLATE 2: SECOND-ORDER EFFECTS MAPPER

### Purpose
Map the cascade from macro trigger to portfolio impact. Forces you to think beyond first-order effects (what's obvious) to second and third-order effects (where alpha lives).

### Structure + Sections

**Section 1: Trigger Input**
- Macro event (same as Detector)
- Public narrative

**Section 2: First-Order Effect**
- What's the obvious market response?
- What's already priced in?
- Who benefits immediately?

**Section 3: Second-Order Effect**
- What happens after the obvious play?
- Who becomes relevant?
- Which industries are affected?
- What prices change next?

**Section 4: Third-Order Effect**
- What happens 6+ months out?
- Ripples from the cascade?
- Which companies are positioned for that?

**Section 5: Companies Affected**
| Category | Companies | Impact |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| **Beneficiaries** | Names | Why they profit |
| **Losers** | Names | Why they suffer |
| **Positioned** | Undervalued names | Why they're undervalued now |

**Section 6: Timeline + Catalyst**
- When does each order of effect unfold?
- What data/news confirms each stage?

**Section 7: Investment Thesis**
- Which companies are undervalued given this cascade?
- What's your entry conviction?

### Example Data (Hormuz → Equipment Replacement)

| Order | Effect | Companies | Timeline |
|-------|--------|-----------|----------|
| **1st** | Oil prices up, shipping costs rise | Energy stocks pop, shipping firms benefit | Week 1–2 (already priced) |
| **2nd** | Fertilizer shortage in regions | Fertilizer companies margin-compressed | Month 2–3 (starting to price) |
| **3rd** | Equipment replacement accelerates | Equipment makers (John Deere, AGCO) boom | Month 4–6 (not priced yet) |

### Walkthrough Guide (3 Paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1: Finding the Second-Order Effect**

First-order effects are obvious. Everyone sees them. Ignore them—they're already priced. Your edge is second-order: What happens after the obvious? Supply shock → shortage → replacement. Each step creates a new opportunity.

**Paragraph 2: Identifying Undervalued Companies**

Once you map the cascade, scan for companies that will profit from each stage. Which ones are cheap relative to what's coming? Which are completely ignored by the market? Those are your entries. Update this section as you research. Add company tickers, valuations, why they're undervalued.

**Paragraph 3: Using Timeline to Manage Positions**

Know when each stage of the cascade unfolds. First-order takes 2–4 weeks. Second-order takes 1–3 months. Third-order takes 6+ months. Size your positions accordingly. Short-term scalps on the cascade stages. Long-term conviction positions on the companies undervalued by stage 3. Exit when the cascade is fully priced.

## TEMPLATE 3: THESIS EXECUTION PLANNER

### Purpose
Document your entry thesis + plan your exits before you buy. Forces discipline through:
- Written thesis statement (why you're buying)
- Entry logic (valuation + momentum)
- OCO structure (stops + targets)
- Actual vs. planned tracking (did thesis validate?)

### Structure + Sections

**Section 1: Thesis Statement**
- One sentence: Exactly why you're buying this company
- Valuation thesis: Is it cheap? Relative to what?
- Catalyst thesis: What will make the market reprrice it?
- Timeline: When does this happen?

**Section 2: Entry Logic**
- Valuation (P/E, Price/Book, DCF fair value)
- Technical confirmation (momentum signal)
- Macro backdrop (how does your macro thesis support this?)
- Conviction weight (1–10, same as Detector)

**Section 3: Position Sizing**
- Conviction weight determines sizing
- Core conviction (8–10): 5–10% of portfolio
- Conviction (6–7): 2–5% of portfolio
- Speculation (4–5): <1% of portfolio

**Section 4: OCO Structure (Orders)**
- **Stop loss:** Where you exit if thesis breaks
- **Target 1 (Scalp exit):** 20–30% position, partial profit-taking
- **Target 2 (Core exit):** 30–40% position, at fair value
- **Target 3 (Conviction exit):** 20–30% position, let it run longer

Example:
```
BUY: $42 (entry price)
STOP: $38 (thesis breaks, -10%)
TARGET 1: $50 (exit 25%, +19% on that tranche)
TARGET 2: $60 (exit 35%, +43% on that tranche)
TARGET 3: $75 (exit 40%, +79% on that tranche)
```

**Section 5: Tax Awareness**
- Entry date (for tax lot tracking)
- Long-term vs. short-term split (when do different tranches convert to long-term?)
- Wash-sale calendar (if you exit and want to re-enter)

**Section 6: Quarterly Thesis Review**
| Quarter | Thesis Status | Market Validation | Adjustment |
|---------|---------------|-------------------|------------|
| Q1 | [Thesis statement] | [Is it unfolding?] | [Hold/scale/exit] |
| Q2 | ... | ... | ... |
| Q3 | ... | ... | ... |

**Section 7: Actual vs. Planned**
| Parameter | Planned | Actual | Variance |
|-----------|---------|--------|----------|
| Entry price | $42 | [actual] | [difference] |
| Exit 1 price | $50 | [actual] | [P&L] |
| Exit 2 price | $60 | [actual] | [P&L] |
| Hold period | 12 months | [actual] | [in progress/closed] |
| Total thesis P&L | [expected] | [actual] | [variance] |

### Example Data (NAT, Nordic American Tankers)

| Field | Data |
|-------|------|
| **Thesis** | Shipping rates are cyclical. At depressed rates, NAT is undervalued relative to normalized earnings. Supply shocks (Hormuz) drive rate spikes. NAT's tanker fleet profits. |
| **Valuation** | P/B = 0.6x (undervalued relative to 1.2x peer average); dividend yield = 8% (compensates for cyclicality) |
| **Catalyst** | Geopolitical supply shock (Hormuz, supertanker rerouting) drives rates up. NAT fleet utilization spikes. Earnings surprise. Multiple re-rates. |
| **Timeline** | Cascade unfolds over 6–12 months. Exit when rates normalize OR thesis breaks. |
| **Conviction** | 7/10 (shipping cycles are reliable, but geopolitical unknowns introduce risk) |
| **Entry** | Buy at $14. Stop at $12. Targets: $16 (25%), $19 (35%), $24 (40%) |
| **Position size** | 4% of portfolio (conviction weight = 7/10) |

### Walkthrough Guide (3 Paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1: Writing Your Thesis Statement**

Don't write a novel. One sentence. "I'm buying NAT because shipping rates are cyclical and low right now; geopolitical supply shocks will spike rates and drive unexpected earnings growth." That's it. That's your thesis. Everything else flows from that. If you can't state it in one sentence, you don't understand why you're buying.

**Paragraph 2: Structure Your OCO Orders Before You Buy**

This is discipline. Calculate your stop (where thesis breaks), your profit-taking targets (where you take partial profits), and your conviction target (where you let it run). OCO = One-Cancels-Other. You set all three upfront. Market fills one, cancels the others. You execute your plan, not your emotions. Don't skip this. Most traders don't do this. That's why most traders fail.

**Paragraph 3: Review Quarterly + Track Actual vs. Planned**

Every quarter, update this template. Is your thesis still valid? Is the market confirming it? Update the "Actual vs. Planned" section so you see how your decisions are working. Did you exit at targets? Did thesis break? Document it. Over time, you'll see patterns: Maybe your targets are too conservative. Maybe your stops are too tight. This data improves your future entries.
